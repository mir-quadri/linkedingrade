The honest grade,
built by hand.

A brand system for linkedingrade.com — three logo proposals, a complete token set, and the imagery the product earns. Editorial in posture, data-dense in detail, restrained on color. No mascots, no gradients, no soft pastels. One red dot is doing most of the work.

§ 01 — LOGO

Three concepts. Three different bets.

Not three variations of one idea — three strategic positions. The Grade owns the product mechanic. The Wordmark trusts the typography. The Index points at the analytical engine. Each ships with a full lockup, a compact mark, and a single-color version that survives embossing.

— RECOMMENDATION

Ship Option B — The Wordmark.

It is the most confident of the three and the one that ages best. A grade-as-mark logo (Option A) trades short-term recognition for a permanent association with school imagery the brand brief explicitly avoids. A bar chart (Option C) is sharp but joins a crowded analytics-tool aesthetic where every fintech and dashboard product already lives. The "linkedin/grade." wordmark — with one red slash and one red period — is unmistakably us, scales from 16px favicon (the "l/g." compact form) to a billboard without modification, and lets the typography carry the brand the way Stripe and Linear let theirs do the work. The mark we save for the favicon: a 32×32 rounded square containing just "l/g." in red.

RADIUS · BORDERS · SHADOWS

Radius

sm 4 buttons, inputs, tags · md 8 nested boxes · lg 14 cards, the audit report, modals. Three values, no more.

Borders

Hairline rgba(text, .09) for structure · strong rgba(text, .18) for cards · full text for masthead rules and section dividers only.

Shadows

Effectively none. One acceptable hairline shadow at 0 1px 0 rgba(text,.04) for the sticky nav lift. Otherwise structure is carried by rules and borders, not depth.

ICONS · LUCIDE · 1.5PX STROKE · ROUND CAPS · OUTLINE ONLY

Lucide, never Phosphor (Phosphor's softer curves fight the typography). 1.5px stroke. Round caps. Outline by default, filled only when active. Never colored unless the action is destructive (then accent) or affirmative (then success, sparingly).

§ 04 — NOTES

Designer's notes. Push-back & sharpening.

Five decisions I'd defend if you asked me to change them, and five places this brief should sharpen before v2. Read as a junior designer talking to a manager who can take it.

Things I'd push back on

  1. Red, not orange or blue.

    Brief said "Economist red equivalent". I picked the deeper #D62828 / #FF4D3B pair instead of a brighter "AI orange" or a safer corporate red. If you tell me to soften it, I'd ship something fewer people remember.

  2. Recommend B (Wordmark) — even though A has the better story.

    Option A is the more "marketable" pitch in a pitch deck (the grade IS the product). It also chains the brand to school imagery for the next decade. B is harder to fall in love with at first and easier to live with for years.

  3. No serif in the product.

    I added Instrument Serif as an editorial flourish on the brand book and in pulled quotes. Inside the product (audit reports, dashboard, extension), it never appears. Mixing serifs into a data-dense product makes it read as a blog.

  4. Lowercase everything, including the wordmark.

    Lowercase is the most opinionated typographic call in the brief. It signals confidence without performance. If you ask me to capital-case "LinkedinGrade" it will read corporate within two weeks.

  5. No hero illustration.

    The hero anchor is the audit card itself, not a stylised mockup. The product is the picture. Putting a "stylised abstract grade circle" next to the headline would be the SaaS-template move the brief explicitly rejects.

Things to sharpen in v2

  1. "Bloomberg meets recruiter gut" is two metaphors.

    One is institutional and quantitative; the other is intuitive and subjective. Pick one for marketing copy and the other for product micro-copy, or write a unifying line. Right now they fight on the home page.

  2. The pricing tiers carry a positioning conflict.

    $19 Pro looks like a consumer SKU; $149 Team looks B2B. There's no Coach-to-Team ladder. Either the Coach plan needs API access (so coaches can grow into Team), or the Pro plan should be priced at $29 and lose CSV export to harden the upsell.

  3. "6-page PDF" is concrete but unchallenged.

    Six is arbitrary. Default to "6-page" in marketing, but ship a version-2 report length tied to seniority — a graduate analyst doesn't need 6 pages, a CEO needs more. The mechanic is grade-density, not page-count.

  4. The free tier has no virality lever.

    A free single audit with a watermarked PDF and no sharing prompt is a missed loop. Add: a public, link-shareable "lite" version with the composite grade only and a "see your full audit" CTA. Watermark the PDF with a per-user share code.

  5. "Reverse-engineer the profiles you compete with" is the strongest line in the brief and isn't the hero.

    The hero says "every profile gets a grade", which is correct but undifferentiated. Test a hero variant that leads with the competitive-intelligence angle. The first audience in the brief (Active job seekers) implies it; the copy doesn't deliver it.
